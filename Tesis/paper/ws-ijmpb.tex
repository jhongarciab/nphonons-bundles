\begin{abstract}
	We study $n$-phonon bundle emission in an excitonic molecule consisting of two identical quantum dots coherently coupled via F\"orster interaction and driven by a laser in resonance with the Stokes sidebands of an acoustic nanocavity. The F\"orster coupling hybridizes the single-exciton manifold into collective bright and dark states, giving rise to superradiant emission dynamics and a characteristic shift of the Stokes resonances absent in the single-emitter case. We characterize the emission statistics through higher-order correlation functions and quantum trajectory simulations, and compute the emission purity $\Pi_n$ across the relevant parameter space. High-purity three-phonon bundle emission is achievable with cavity quality factors up to five times lower than those required in the single-emitter case, a relaxation that originates directly from the superradiant amplification of the emission cascade and renders the system inherently more robust against cavity losses. The predicted parameters are consistent with state-of-the-art semiconductor quantum dot nanocavity platforms, establishing the excitonic molecule as a viable route toward high-purity multi-phonon quantum acoustics.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{phonon bundle emission, excitonic molecule, F\"orster coupling, superradiance, cavity QED, emission purity}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:int}

Quantum vibrational modes of solids, described by phonons, have a great potential to be used in 
technological applications in quantum metrology, quantum sensing, and on-chip quantum 
communications~\cite{balandin2005,manenti2017,schuetz2015,gustafsson2014,noguchi2017,arrangoiz2018}. 
Analogous to photons in quantum electrodynamics, phonons can be used to store, process, and 
transduce quantum information, with a three-fold advantage over their photonic counterparts: 
acoustic waves propagate at speeds orders of magnitude slower than light, making them especially 
suited for short-distance on-chip communication~\cite{gustafsson2014,kuzyk2018}; phonons are 
confined to material media and thus immune to radiation losses into vacuum; and acoustic cavities are highly tunable, with experimental demonstrations spanning from gigahertz 
to terahertz frequencies~\cite{weig2004,rozas2009,oconnell2010,macCabe2020,bolgar2018,chu2017,zalalutdinov2021,soykal2011,fainstein2013,kharel2018,gokhale2020}.
As the control of individual phonons continues to improve, many-phonon states are increasingly 
required for highly nonclassical sources, useful for quantum sensing and 
metrology~\cite{toyoda2015,zhang2018,chu2018,satzinger2018}, phononic quantum 
networks~\cite{habraken2012,calajo2019,lemonde2018}, and quantum memories and 
transducers~\cite{schuetz2015,arrangoiz2018,bienfait2019}. The controlled generation of 
high-quality multi-phonon states has therefore become a central task of quantum phononics.

In the photonic domain, multiphoton bundle emission has been explored in cavity QED 
settings~\cite{kubanek2008,ota2011,gonzaleztudela2013}, and a scheme for the direct generation 
of $n$-photon bundles, states in which $n$ photons are emitted simultaneously into the same 
mode, was proposed by Mu\~noz et al.~\cite{munoz2014} and later extended to the bad-cavity 
regime through spectral filtering~\cite{munoz2018,chang2016}. The phononic analogue was 
introduced by Bin et al.~\cite{bin2020}, who demonstrated theoretically that a single quantum dot 
(QD) coupled to an acoustic nanocavity and coherently driven by a laser at the $n$th-order Stokes 
sideband can emit $n$-phonon bundles of high purity. The mechanism relies on super-Rabi 
oscillations~\cite{strekalov2014} between states differing by $n$ phonons, induced by the 
nonlinear electron-phonon interaction: unlike the photonic leapfrog mechanism of 
Ref.~\cite{munoz2014}, which exploits Jaynes-Cummings-enhanced multiphoton transitions, the 
phononic Stokes process accompanies each QD flip with the coherent generation of $n$ phonons 
directly in the cavity mode. Pure bundle emission is then achieved by opening a dissipative channel 
that converts these oscillations into correlated phonon emission toward the 
exterior~\cite{bin2020,vargas2022}. This scheme is optically tunable and achieves near-unity 
purity over a wide parameter range; however, it places demanding requirements on the cavity 
quality factor $Q$, which must remain sufficiently high to preserve coherence throughout the 
multi-phonon cascade, a constraint that limits the practical accessibility of the proposal with 
current acoustic nanocavity technology~\cite{wigger2021,kettler2021,macCabe2020}.

A natural extension is to replace the single emitter with an excitonic molecule formed by two 
quantum dots coherently coupled via F\"orster 
interaction~\cite{forster1948,kagan1996,crooker2002}. This resonant energy transfer mechanism 
between quantum dots has been widely studied in semiconductor 
systems~\cite{kagan1996,crooker2002}, and recent work has shown that a shared phononic 
environment between coupled QDs introduces nontrivial many-body effects on the system's 
decoherence dynamics~\cite{hall2025}. The F\"orster coupling hybridizes the single-exciton 
manifold into bright and dark states, enabling superradiant emission dynamics and a 
characteristic shift of the Stokes resonances with no counterpart in the single-emitter case. 
Such phenomena have been observed in solid-state quantum emitters coupled to 
nanophotonic waveguides~\cite{tiranov2023}, where both super- and subradiant emission have been 
identified and controlled. In multi-emitter phononic systems, analogous symmetry-breaking 
mechanisms have been shown to modify spontaneous emission rates~\cite{kockum2014} and amplify 
phonon lasing~\cite{kabuss2012,droenner2017}. The bright state, in particular, amplifies the 
effective super-Rabi frequency by a superradiant factor, accelerating the phonon emission 
cascade and reducing the sensitivity of the bundle purity to cavity losses, potentially 
allowing high-purity multi-phonon emission in the bad-cavity limit, i.e., with quality factors 
far below those required in the single-emitter scheme of Bin et al.~\cite{bin2020}.

In this work we extend the scheme of Bin et al.~\cite{bin2020} to the excitonic molecule and 
determine the conditions under which F\"orster coupling yields high-purity $n$-phonon bundle 
emission in the bad-cavity limit. We characterize the collective Stokes resonance structure and 
the super-Rabi dynamics in the weak coupling and weak driving regime, analyze the $n$-phonon 
emission statistics through higher-order correlation functions and quantum trajectory simulations, 
and compute emission purity maps $\Pi_n$~\cite{munoz2014,munoz2018} across the relevant 
parameter space. The paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:model} presents the model 
and theoretical framework; Section~\ref{sec:results} discusses the main results; and 
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the conclusions.

\section{Model and Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}

We consider an excitonic molecule formed by two identical quantum dots, QD$_1$ and QD$_2$,
each modeled as a two-level system with valence state $|v\rangle$, conduction state $|c\rangle$,
transition frequency $\omega_\sigma$, and electron-phonon coupling strength $\lambda$. Both dots
interact with a single confined phonon mode of an acoustic nanocavity of frequency $\omega_b$,
and are coherently coupled to each other via the F\"orster interaction with constant $J$. An
external coherent laser of frequency $\omega_L$ and Rabi frequency $\Omega$ drives both dots
symmetrically. Defining the individual exciton operators $\sigma_i = |v\rangle_i\langle c|$ and
the total electronic number operator $\hat{N} = \sigma_1^\dagger\sigma_1 +
\sigma_2^\dagger\sigma_2$, the total Hamiltonian reads ($\hbar = 1$)

\begin{equation}
	H_\mathrm{lab} = \omega_b b^\dagger b + \omega_\sigma \hat{N} + \lambda\,\hat{N}(b^\dagger + b)
	+ J\!\left(\sigma_1^\dagger\sigma_2 + \sigma_2^\dagger\sigma_1\right)
	+ \Omega\sum_{i=1,2}\!\left(e^{i\omega_L t}\sigma_i
	+ e^{-i\omega_L t}\sigma_i^\dagger\right).
	\label{eq:Hlab}
\end{equation}

Moving to the rotating frame with detuning $\Delta \equiv \omega_\sigma - \omega_L$, the
Hamiltonian becomes

\begin{equation}
	H = \omega_b b^\dagger b + \Delta\,\hat{N} + \lambda\,\hat{N}(b^\dagger + b)
	+ J\!\left(\sigma_1^\dagger\sigma_2 + \sigma_2^\dagger\sigma_1\right)
	+ \Omega\sum_{i=1,2}\!\left(\sigma_i + \sigma_i^\dagger\right).
	\label{eq:H}
\end{equation}

The F\"orster term conserves $\hat{N}$ and is time-independent in the rotating frame.
Diagonalizing it within the single-excitation subspace yields the symmetric bright state
$|\Psi_+\rangle = (|cv\rangle + |vc\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$ with energy $+J$ and the antisymmetric
dark state $|\Psi_-\rangle = (|cv\rangle - |vc\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$ with energy $-J$. Since
$\Omega(\sigma_1^\dagger + \sigma_2^\dagger)|vv\rangle = \sqrt{2}\,\Omega\,|\Psi_+\rangle$,
the laser couples exclusively to $|\Psi_+\rangle$ with collective Rabi frequency
$\sqrt{2}\,\Omega$, while $|\Psi_-\rangle$ remains dark and acquires population only through
individual pure dephasing. Introducing the collective operators $S^\dagger =
|\Psi_+\rangle\langle vv|$ and $S = |vv\rangle\langle\Psi_+|$ and restricting the dynamics to
the bright subspace $\{|vv\rangle, |\Psi_+\rangle\}$, the Hamiltonian takes the effective form

\begin{equation}
	H_\mathrm{eff} = \omega_b b^\dagger b + (\Delta + J)\,S^\dagger S
	+ \lambda\,S^\dagger S(b^\dagger + b) + \sqrt{2}\,\Omega\!\left(S + S^\dagger\right).
	\label{eq:Heff}
\end{equation}

In the weak coupling and weak driving regime $\Omega, \lambda \ll \omega_b$, the system exhibits
$n$-phonon Stokes resonances when states $|0, vv\rangle$ and $|n, \Psi_+\rangle$ become
degenerate, which occurs at

\begin{equation}
	\Delta_n = -n\omega_b - J.
	\label{eq:res}
\end{equation}

Applying the L\"owdin partitioning method~\cite{lowdin1951} to eliminate the $2n$ intermediate
states, the effective coupling between $|0, vv\rangle$ and $|n, \Psi_+\rangle$ is

\begin{equation}
	\Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)} = \sqrt{2}\left(\frac{\lambda}{\omega_b}\right)^{\!n}
	\frac{\Omega}{\sqrt{n!}},
	\label{eq:Oeff}
\end{equation}

which defines the collective super-Rabi frequency of the $n$-phonon process. The sweet-spot
condition for bundle emission, $\kappa \sim \Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)}$, ensures that cavity
dissipation extracts the correlated phonon cascade before off-resonance processes contaminate it. All numerical results were obtained using QuTiP~\cite{johansson2011}.

\label{sec:results}

Dissipation is necessary to drive phonon bundle emission out of the cavity. The open-system 
dynamics is governed by the Lindblad master equation

\begin{equation}
	\dot{\rho} = -i[H_\mathrm{eff},\rho]
	+ \kappa\,\mathcal{D}[b]\,\rho
	+ \gamma\sum_{i=1,2}\mathcal{D}[\sigma_i]\,\rho
	+ \gamma_\varphi\sum_{i=1,2}\mathcal{D}[\sigma_i^\dagger\sigma_i]\,\rho,
	\label{eq:lindblad}
\end{equation}

where $\kappa$ is the phonon cavity decay rate, $\gamma$ the spontaneous emission rate of each 
dot, and $\gamma_\varphi$ the individual pure dephasing rate. A first signature of the emitted 
field correlations is provided by the equal-time $n$-th order correlation function ~\cite{glauber1963}
$g^{(n)}(0) = \langle b^{\dagger n}b^n\rangle/\langle b^\dagger b\rangle^n$. 
Fig.~\ref{fig:gn} shows $g^{(n)}(0)$ as a function of $\Delta/\omega_b$ for $n = 2$ and $n = 3$. 
Both panels display strong super-bunching peaks at the Stokes resonances 
$\Delta_n = -n\omega_b - J$~\eqref{eq:res}, confirming that the phonon field is dominated by 
correlated $n$-phonon events at the predicted detunings. Notably, each resonance exhibits a 
pronounced dip at its center rather than a smooth maximum: at exact resonance, the $n$ phonons 
are emitted as an indivisible bundle, so single-phonon detection events are no longer 
statistically independent and $g^{(n)}(0)$ loses its ability to capture the relevant bundle 
statistics~\cite{munoz2014}. The uniform spacing $\omega_b$ between consecutive resonances, 
independent of $n$, is a direct signature of the Stokes process: each additional phonon costs 
exactly $\omega_b$ in energy regardless of the bundle order.

To properly characterize the bundle statistics, we use the $m$-th order $n$-bundle correlation 
function~\cite{munoz2014}, whose second-order case reads

\begin{equation}
	g_2^{(2)}(\tau) = \frac{\langle b^{\dagger 2}(0)\,b^{\dagger 2}(\tau)\,
		b^2(\tau)\,b^2(0)\rangle}
	{\langle (b^{\dagger 2}b^2 (0))\rangle\,\langle (b^{\dagger 2}b^2(\tau))\rangle},
	\label{eq:g2bundle}
\end{equation}

which distinguishes sub-Poissonian ($g_2^{(2)} < 1$), Poissonian ($g_2^{(2)} = 1$), and 
super-Poissonian ($g_2^{(2)} > 1$) bundle statistics for $\tau \gg \kappa^{-1}$, corresponding 
to the $n$-phonon gun, $n$-phonon laser, and thermal bundle regimes, respectively. Fig.~\ref{fig:g2_decay} shows $g_2^{(2)}(\tau)$ as a function of $\gamma/\omega_b$ and 
$\omega_b\tau$ at the two-phonon Stokes resonance for two cavity decay rates. In 
panel~(a), for $\gamma \ll \kappa$ the function decays from super-bunching at short times 
$\tau \lesssim \kappa^{-1}$ to $g_2^{(2)} \approx 0.35$ at long times, realizing the 
$n$-phonon gun regime. As $\gamma$ increases toward $\kappa$, $g_2^{(2)}(\tau\to\infty) \to 1$ 
and the emission becomes Poissonian, corresponding to the $n$-phonon laser. For 
$\gamma \gg \kappa$, strong bunching persists at all times, signaling the thermal bundle regime. 
In panel~(b), the larger cavity decay rate drives $g_2^{(2)}(\tau\to\infty) \to 1$ across the 
entire range of $\gamma$, so the $n$-phonon laser regime is reached regardless of the 
spontaneous emission rate. All three regimes are accessible in both panels by tuning 
$\gamma/\kappa$, confirming that the bundle emission statistics are experimentally controllable.

The bundle emission mechanism is directly visualized through Monte Carlo quantum trajectory 
simulations, which track individual system evolution and record phonon clicks at each quantum 
jump in the cavity channel. Fig.~\ref{fig:trayectoria} shows a representative trajectory 
segment at the two-phonon Stokes resonance. The system spends most of its time in the ground 
state $|vv,0\rangle$ ($\langle P_{vv,0}\rangle \approx 0.97$, panel~(a)), while super-Rabi 
oscillations coherently build population in $|\Psi_+,2\rangle$ up to $\sim 4\%$ (panel~(c)), 
with $|\Psi_+,1\rangle$ acting as the intermediate state of the cascade (panel~(b)). When a 
quantum jump occurs in the phonon channel, the first click projects the system onto 
$|\Psi_+,1\rangle$ with near-unit probability, as confirmed by the peak 
$P_{\Psi_+,1} \approx 0.93$ in panel~(b). The second phonon is emitted on a timescale much 
shorter than the cavity lifetime $\kappa^{-1} = 2000\,\omega_b^{-1}$: both clicks occur within 
a window of $\Delta\tau \approx 169\,\omega_b^{-1} \approx 0.08\,\kappa^{-1}$, confirming the 
strongly correlated two-phonon cascade. The dark state $|\Psi_-\rangle$ remains essentially 
unpopulated throughout (maximum $P_{\Psi_-} \approx 9\times10^{-3}$), validating that the 
dynamics is effectively confined to the bright subspace.

The role of individual pure dephasing $\gamma_\varphi$ deserves attention in the two-dot 
context. In the single-emitter case, pure dephasing broadens the Stokes resonances without 
activating additional emission channels. In the excitonic molecule, however, $\gamma_\varphi$ 
acts individually on each dot and therefore breaks the symmetry of the collective subspace, 
transferring population from $|\Psi_+\rangle$ to $|\Psi_-\rangle$ at a rate proportional to 
$\gamma_\varphi$. Since $|\Psi_-\rangle$ is decoupled from the laser, this population transfer 
represents a loss channel with no phononic output, introducing a source of purity degradation 
absent in the single-emitter scheme. The trajectory confirms that this effect remains negligible 
for the parameters considered, but it sets a fundamental limit on bundle purity as 
$\gamma_\varphi$ increases, a constraint intrinsic to any collective two-emitter platform.

After emission, the system returns to $|vv,0\rangle$ via an optical photon from the transition 
$|\Psi_+,0\rangle \to |vv,0\rangle$. Since this photon carries optical frequency distinct from 
$\omega_b$, it can be detected in an independent channel without disturbing the phonon bundle, 
providing a heralding signal for quantum information protocols~\cite{bin2020}.

\subsection{Discussion of Purity and Experimental Feasibility}

In any realistic implementation, $n$-phonon emission is contaminated by off-resonance processes 
of order $m \neq n$. To quantify the source quality we use the emission purity $\Pi_n$, 
introduced by Mu\~noz et al.~\cite{munoz2014,munoz2018} as the fraction of the steady-state 
phonon population attributable exclusively to the resonant $n$-phonon process. It is computed 
via the two-steady-state method, solving the system at Fock space truncations $N_\mathrm{cut}$ 
and $N_\mathrm{cut} - n$,

\begin{equation}
	\Pi_n = \frac{n\,\langle\hat{n}_b\rangle^{(n)}_\mathrm{trunc}}
	{\langle\hat{n}_b\rangle_\mathrm{full}},
	\label{eq:purity}
\end{equation}

where $\langle\hat{n}_b\rangle_\mathrm{full}$ is the mean phonon number in the full space and 
$\langle\hat{n}_b\rangle^{(n)}_\mathrm{trunc}$ is the contribution isolated by the truncation.

Fig.~\ref{fig:purity_2qd_heatmap} shows the purity maps for $n = 2, 3, 4$ in the 
$(\lambda/\omega_b, \kappa/\omega_b)$ parameter space. For $n = 2$, the high-purity region 
($\Pi_2 \gtrsim 0.90$) spans approximately half the explored space; as the order increases, 
this region progressively narrows to a strip at small $\kappa$ and large $\lambda$ for $n = 4$. 
This narrowing reflects the $\Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)} \propto (\lambda/\omega_b)^n$ dependence: 
higher-order processes require stronger coupling to dominate over off-resonance emission, 
shifting the optimal purity point toward larger $\lambda$ with increasing $n$, from 
$\lambda/\omega_b \sim 0.08$ for $n = 2$ to $\lambda/\omega_b \approx 0.22$ for $n = 3$, where 
$\Pi_3 \approx 98.9\%$ and $\Pi_4 \approx 92.5\%$ are simultaneously accessible at 
$\kappa/\omega_b \sim 10^{-3}$. The purity is also sensitive to $\kappa$: for 
$\lambda/\omega_b = 0.22$, $\Pi_3$ drops from $\approx 99\%$ to $\approx 97\%$ as 
$\kappa/\omega_b$ increases from $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-2}$, while $\Pi_4$ falls more severely 
from $92\%$ to $62\%$ over the same range, since higher-order cascades offer more channels 
for off-resonance contamination before the bundle is completed.

It is worth noting that the F\"orster coupling $J$ enters the purity maps only indirectly 
through the resonance shift $\Delta_n = -n\omega_b - J$~\eqref{eq:res}: once the laser is 
tuned to the shifted resonance, $J$ does not appear explicitly in $\Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)}$ 
and therefore does not alter the purity landscape, making the maps in 
Fig.~\ref{fig:purity_2qd_heatmap} robust against variations in the F\"orster constant. The 
practical implication is that $J$ serves as a spectroscopic handle; it shifts the operating 
frequency of the source without degrading its purity, which is advantageous for frequency 
multiplexing in phononic quantum networks where multiple sources must operate at distinct but 
well-defined acoustic frequencies.

Throughout this work we use parameters of the same order of magnitude as those of 
Ref.~\cite{bin2020}, which allows a direct comparison of the phonon bundle statistics and 
purity between the single-emitter and excitonic molecule schemes. For 
$\omega_b/2\pi = 1\,\mathrm{THz}$, these correspond to $\Omega/2\pi = 0.2\,\mathrm{THz}$, 
$\gamma/2\pi = 0.2\,\mathrm{GHz}$, and $\gamma_\varphi/2\pi = 0.4\,\mathrm{GHz}$, consistent 
with experimentally reported values in semiconductor QD-nanocavity 
systems~\cite{weig2004,rozas2009,borri2001}. The value $\lambda/\omega_b = 0.22$ used in the 
purity optimization represents a plausible scenario: the Weig et al.\ system exhibits 
Huang-Rhys factors $S \sim 1.7$--$2.5$~\cite{weig2004}, equivalent under 
$S = (\lambda/\omega_b)^2$ to $\lambda/\omega_b \sim 1.3$--$1.6$, confirming that 
dimensionless couplings well above the conservative reference $\lambda/\omega_b = 0.14$ of 
Ref.~\cite{bin2020} are physically attainable.

The cavity quality factor requirement is where the excitonic molecule offers its most 
significant practical advantage. Rozas et al.\ demonstrate that $Q = \omega_b/\kappa$ in 
$1\,\mathrm{THz}$ acoustic nanocavities is limited primarily by thickness fluctuations at the 
$1/10$ unit-cell scale~\cite{rozas2009}, placing realistic fabrication constraints in the 
range $Q \sim 90$--$300$. Consistently, free-decay measurements in high-frequency 2D acoustic 
cavities report $Q \sim 140$ at $70\,\mathrm{GHz}$~\cite{zalalutdinov2021}, establishing a 
direct experimental precedent for quality factors in this range. Under these conditions, at 
$\lambda/\omega_b = 0.22$ the theory predicts $\Pi_2 \approx 98.6\%$ and 
$\Pi_3 \approx 98.9\%$ at $\kappa/2\pi \approx 1\,\mathrm{GHz}$ ($Q \sim 900$), with 
$\Pi_3 > 97\%$ maintained up to $\kappa/2\pi \approx 7.6\,\mathrm{GHz}$ ($Q \sim 130$) and 
$\Pi_3 > 95\%$ up to $\kappa/2\pi \approx 11\,\mathrm{GHz}$ ($Q \sim 90$). The single-emitter 
scheme of Bin et al.\ requires $Q \sim 500$ to reach the same $\Pi_3 \approx 95\%$ 
threshold~\cite{bin2020}; the excitonic molecule achieves it with cavities up to five times 
less demanding. The physical origin is the superradiant factor $\sqrt{2}$ amplifying 
$\Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)}$: the emission cascade completes more rapidly, reducing the time 
window available for off-resonance processes to contaminate the bundle before the cavity 
releases it. This establishes the F\"orster-coupled excitonic molecule as an experimentally 
viable platform for high-purity multi-phonon quantum acoustics with current 
technology~\cite{weig2004,rozas2009,zalalutdinov2021,macCabe2020}.


\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We have studied $n$-phonon bundle emission in a F\"orster-coupled excitonic molecule and shown 
that the collective hybridization into bright and dark states modifies the emission dynamics 
qualitatively with respect to the single-emitter case. The superradiant factor $\sqrt{2}$ in 
the effective super-Rabi frequency and the rigid shift $-J$ of the Stokes resonances are 
structural consequences of the F\"orster coupling that determine both the spectral position of 
the operating points and the timescale of the phonon emission cascade.

The central result is that three-phonon bundle purity $\Pi_3 > 95\%$ is achievable with 
cavity quality factors $Q \sim 90$--$130$, compared to $Q \sim 500$ required in the 
single-emitter scheme. This fivefold relaxation originates from the superradiant amplification 
of $\Omega_\mathrm{eff}^{(n)}$: the faster cascade reduces the time window available for 
off-resonance contamination, rendering the system inherently more robust against cavity losses. 
The F\"orster constant $J$ acts as a spectroscopic handle that shifts the operating frequency 
without altering the purity landscape, a feature exploitable for frequency multiplexing in 
phononic quantum networks. The bundle statistics are tunable across the $n$-phonon gun, 
$n$-phonon laser, and thermal bundle regimes by adjusting $\gamma/\kappa$, and the predicted 
parameters are consistent with state-of-the-art semiconductor QD-nanocavity platforms.
